Limitations of the Study

While the quantitative approach provides clear evidence for the discursive contrast between Ku Hung‑ming and Lin Yutang, several limitations must be acknowledged.

1. Word frequency ignores context and nuance

The method treats every occurrence of a keyword equally, regardless of its rhetorical function. For example, “moral” used in a sarcastic or critical passage is counted the same as in an affirmative statement. A more fine‑grained analysis would require manual annotation of semantic prosody and collocation patterns.

2. Limited to two English works

The study only examines The Spirit of the Chinese People and My Country and My People. It does not analyse Ku’s other English writings (e.g., his translations of Confucian classics) nor Lin’s later works such as The Importance of Living. Including more texts would test whether the observed strategies are consistent across each author’s oeuvre.

3. No comparison with Chinese‑language writings

Both authors also wrote extensively in Chinese. Comparing their English works with their Chinese writings could reveal how language choice affects their discursive strategies. For instance, does Ku adopt a different posture when addressing a Chinese audience? This remains unexplored.

4. Pronoun ratio simplification

The we/they ratio does not distinguish between inclusive “we” (including the reader) and exclusive “we” (referring only to the author’s group). A detailed manual annotation would be needed to refine this measure. However, the large difference between the two authors (2.7 vs 0.71) is unlikely to be reversed by such refinement.

5. Absence of domestic‑audience comparison

The study does not systematically compare Ku and Lin with writers who addressed Chinese readers, such as Lu Xun’s critical essays on national character. Such a comparison would help isolate the effect of the intended audience on discursive strategies.

These limitations do not invalidate the findings, but they suggest that the quantitative results should be interpreted alongside close reading and that further research can build on this foundation.

Future Research Directions

The methods developed in this study can be extended in multiple ways to deepen our understanding of early 20th‑century Chinese intellectual discourse.

1. Collocation and semantic prosody

Instead of only counting frequencies, future studies could analyse the typical neighbours of keywords. For example, what adjectives or verbs co‑occur most frequently with “moral” in Ku’s text? Does “moral” tend to appear with defensive terms like “defend”, “superior”, “against”, or with descriptive terms? Such collocation analysis would reveal the evaluative meaning of keywords.

2. Word embeddings and semantic shifts

Using word embedding models (e.g., Word2Vec), researchers can trace how concepts such as “civilisation”, “China”, or “people” shift in meaning over time or differ between authors. This could uncover subtle ideological differences that raw frequencies miss.

3. Expanding the corpus

Include more English works by Chinese intellectuals from the same period, such as Chen Jitong’s Les Chinois peints par eux‑mêmes (in French) or later works by Hu Shih and Liang Qichao. A larger corpus would allow generalisations about the “defence vs empathy” framework across different authors.

4. Comparative analysis with Chinese domestic critiques

Applying the same quantitative methods to Lu Xun’s Chinese essays (e.g., Diary of a Madman, The True Story of Ah Q) would measure how the same author positions himself toward a domestic audience. The we/they ratio, for instance, might be very different when the target is fellow Chinese citizens rather than Westerners.

5. Cross‑linguistic comparison with translations

Compare the original English texts with their Chinese translations to examine how the author‑reader relationship shifts when the text is re‑presented to a different linguistic community. This could reveal the extent to which discursive strategies are language‑dependent.

These extensions would further test the “audience effect” hypothesis and contribute to a digital history of cross‑cultural intellectual exchange.

Conclusion: Defence or Empathy?

This study set out to answer a simple question: how did two leading Chinese intellectuals, writing for the same English‑speaking audience, construct such different images of the Chinese people? Using keyword frequency analysis, chapter distribution measurement, and pronoun ratio calculation, the thesis has provided quantifiable evidence for a contrast that has long been noted in qualitative criticism.

Summary of findings
Ku Hung‑ming’s The Spirit of the Chinese People is dominated by abstract moral vocabulary (“moral”, “civilisation”), with 70.4% of “moral” occurrences packed into the first chapter. This deductive, theory‑first structure and a low we/they ratio (0.71) signal a defensive discourse: Ku builds a moral fortress to shield Chinese civilisation from Western criticism, addressing the reader as an adversary or at least a sceptic.

Lin Yutang’s My Country and My People, in contrast, relies on concrete nouns (“Chinese”, “people”, “life”) distributed evenly across chapters. His inductive, evidence‑accumulating style and a high we/they ratio (2.7) indicate an empathetic discourse. Lin invites the reader to observe China as an everyday world, using inclusive language to diminish the distance between author and audience.

Why did they differ?
The difference stems from divergent assumptions about the Western reader. Ku imagined a biased critic who needed to be refuted; Lin imagined a curious observer who needed information and understanding. Both strategies were responses to the same historical condition – Western dominance and Chinese weakness – but they chose opposite rhetorical paths. Neither is inherently superior; each reflects a different conception of the intellectual’s role in cross‑cultural dialogue.

Contribution of digital methods
This study demonstrates that computational text analysis can reveal argumentative structures that close reading alone tends to miss. Word frequencies, chapter distributions, and pronoun ratios are not mere “data”; they are traces of strategic choices made by authors. By quantifying these traces, digital humanities tools offer a replicable, transparent layer of evidence that can complement and sharpen interpretation.

Final word
Ku Hung‑ming built a fortress of moral civilisation. Lin Yutang built a bridge of everyday empathy. Both were trying to answer the same urgent question: how to present China to the West under unequal power. The answer is not a single “correct” image, but a spectrum of strategies – and this thesis has measured two points on that spectrum with precision.
