Ku Hung‑ming · Moral Fortress

Interpretation: 70.4% of moral terms appear in Chapter 1. Civilisation peaks in Preface, Introduction, Ch1. This deductive structure proves Ku’s theory‑first argumentation: he establishes an unshakeable moral premise at the outset and only then applies it to specific topics.

Lin Yutang · Everyday Empathy

Interpretation: Moral terms are distributed evenly; the highest share in Chapter 1 is only about 25%. This inductive, evidence‑accumulating structure allows readers to enter at any point without accepting a single theoretical anchor. It reflects Lin’s observational, non‑polemical style.

Side‑by‑Side Word Clouds – Lexical Priorities

Ku Hung‑ming’s cloud is dominated by abstract, moral‑centered terms: moral, civilisation, spirit, character, life. This reflects his defensive strategy of elevating the discussion to the level of moral values, avoiding concrete critiques.

Lin Yutang’s cloud is filled with concrete, people‑oriented nouns: Chinese, people, life, man, woman. These terms ground the text in everyday experience and invite the reader to see China as a relatable human society.

The visual difference confirms the quantitative frequency data and supports the central claim that Ku writes to defend, while Lin writes to invite understanding.

Heatmap Comparison – Argument Structure

Ku’s heatmap shows a single dark column in Chapter 1 for “moral”, indicating extreme concentration. Civilisation shows three peaks (Preface, Introduction, Ch1) but then drops to near zero. This confirms a deductive architecture: the moral premise is fully established at the beginning, and later chapters only apply it.

Lin’s heatmap displays colour spread across many chapters. Moral terms appear consistently without any single chapter dominating. This inductive pattern allows evidence to accumulate gradually, mirroring a conversational, empirical approach.

The contrast visualises argumentative logic: Ku builds a fortress; Lin builds a path.

Contrast Bar Chart – Who Uses Which Words More?

The left panel lists the top ten words that Lin Yutang uses significantly more often than Ku: Chinese, people, life, man, woman, west, confucius, nation, family, character. These are concrete, social, and everyday terms.

The right panel shows the only two words where Ku exceeds Lin: moral and civilisation. The stark asymmetry (10 vs 2) quantitatively proves that the two authors operate in almost completely different lexical universes.

This chart is the most direct evidence that Ku’s discourse is abstract‑defensive, while Lin’s is concrete‑empathetic.

Audience Positioning – We/They Ratio

Lin Yutang’s we/they ratio is 2.7, nearly four times Ku’s 0.71. This means Lin uses first‑person plural pronouns (“we”, “our”, “us”) much more frequently relative to third‑person plural pronouns (“they”, “them”, “their”).

A high “we” frequency indicates that the author places himself on the same side as the reader, building an empathetic bond. Lin writes as if he and the reader are exploring China together. A low ratio, as in Ku’s case, signals a critical distance: Ku addresses the reader as an adversary or an observer from outside.

This quantitative measure directly supports the argument that Lin’s strategy is inclusive and inviting, while Ku’s is defensive and distanced.
